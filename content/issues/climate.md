---
title: "Effective and Lasting Climate Policy"
description: "Why Virginia's 10th District needs a bipartisan climate bill, not a partisan one."
---

Climate policy tends to get presented as a binary choice: either you believe
in aggressive government action to eliminate fossil fuels as fast as
possible, or you're a climate skeptic standing in the way of progress. That
binary doesn't reflect where most Americans, or most serious energy
experts, are.

## Why Fossil Fuels Aren't Going Away on Their Own Timeline

Here is a fact that should inform how we think about this problem: even
China, the country investing more aggressively in solar, wind, and battery
storage than any other nation on earth, still generates more than half of
its electricity from fossil fuels today. That tells us the technology to
fully replace fossil fuels at the scale a modern industrial economy
requires isn't quite there yet, anywhere in the world. It doesn't let the
United States off the hook. We built our economy first and emitted more,
cumulatively and per person, than any country on earth to get here, and
that history means we have a responsibility to lead on bringing the next
generation of clean technology to scale, not an excuse to wait for someone
else to solve it first.

Battery chemistry, grid storage, nuclear power, and other technologies are
advancing quickly, and I support pushing that progress as hard as we can
because a serious energy policy must be built for the world we live in
today while actively building the one we need for tomorrow.

## Why This Has to Be Bipartisan

None of this is simple, and none of the tradeoffs are easy. Moving too
aggressively away from fossil fuels before reliable alternatives exist at
scale risks energy price spikes, grid instability, and real economic pain
for working families. Moving too slowly risks the consequences of unchecked
climate change continuing to compound. Getting that balance right requires
genuinely careful, technical, good-faith judgment — not a bill written by
one party to satisfy its own base, then reversed by the other party as soon
as power changes hands. A bipartisan bill, built by members of both parties
who have to actually defend it to their own constituents, is far more
likely to land on tradeoffs that can survive a change in political power,
and far more likely to get the engineering judgment right rather than the
ideological talking point right.

## The Permitting Problem: Where Bipartisanship Is Already Happening

Here's something most voters don't know: the federal approval process for
any major energy project — a new transmission line, a solar farm, a
pipeline, a nuclear plant, a geothermal well — is governed by a 1970 law
called the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA). Today, it routinely
takes federal agencies four and a half years on average just to complete
the environmental review for a major energy project, and legal challenges
can add years more on top of that. This slows down everything: clean energy
projects and fossil fuel projects alike. It is not a partisan problem. It
is an infrastructure problem, and it is hindering our ability to build
anything quickly in this country, at exactly the moment electricity demand
is surging because of AI data centers, reshored manufacturing, and
electrification.

The good news is that permitting reform is one of the few issues in
Washington right now where genuine bipartisan work is already happening,
and it deserves more attention than it's getting. The House passed the
SPEED Act in December 2025, and several similar bipartisan bills are moving
alongside it in both chambers. None of these have been signed into law yet,
and as written, they're built to be strictly technology-neutral: treating a
pipeline and a solar farm the same. I don't think that's the right goal. At
a moment when electricity demand is surging and the clean energy industry
is the one facing the worst project delays, technology-neutral reform
mostly locks in the fossil fuel industry's existing advantage rather than
closing the gap. I would amend these bills to tilt permitting reform
deliberately toward clean energy: fund federal agencies to clear their
review backlogs, and fix the single biggest bottleneck holding back new
clean energy specifically — transmission. Today, a solar or wind project can
be fully built and ready to generate power, only to sit stalled for years
waiting on a transmission line to connect it to the grid. I support federal
backstop siting authority and dedicated funding to get interstate
transmission lines built faster, and a dedicated fast-track review lane for
new clean energy generation and transmission projects. Fossil fuel projects
would continue through the same review process and timelines in place
today — no rollback of environmental standards, but no new fast lane
either. The point of permitting reform should be to help build the energy
system we need for the future, not to hand fossil fuel infrastructure an
equal seat at the front of the line.

## What I Support

I support a bipartisan climate bill that:

- Aggressively expands clean and renewable energy — solar, wind, nuclear,
  geothermal, and next-generation battery storage — wherever it can be
  deployed at reasonable cost, without compromising grid reliability, and
  without compromising public safety and health.
- Acknowledges that fossil fuels will remain part of the energy mix for the
  foreseeable future and takes a pragmatic, technology-neutral approach to
  reducing their impact over time, rather than locking in a rigid timeline
  that ignores real-world engineering and economic constraints.
- Invests in the mineral supply chains and domestic manufacturing capacity
  that any serious clean energy transition will require, so we're not
  trading dependence on oil for dependence on foreign-controlled battery
  minerals.
- Is written and negotiated by members of both parties, so that the
  tradeoffs it makes reflect a durable consensus rather than a temporary
  majority — and so the resulting policy survives long enough for
  businesses and families to plan around it.
- Includes real permitting reform, building on the bipartisan momentum
  already underway in Congress, by funding federal agencies to clear their
  review backlogs and creating a dedicated fast lane for the transmission
  lines and clean energy projects stuck waiting years to connect to the
  grid, so approved projects can't be cancelled by political whiplash after
  developers have already invested.
- Protects the communities that have carried the heaviest burden of
  pollution from power plants, refineries, and industrial facilities, by
  preserving full environmental review and community input for new fossil
  fuel infrastructure and directing dedicated funding toward air and water
  quality improvements in the frontline neighborhoods most affected.
- Backs the workers who built our energy industry with real transition
  support — job retraining, wage replacement, and pension protection — for
  coal, oil, and gas workers as the energy mix changes, so the cost of that
  change doesn't fall on the workers and families who had no say in it.
- Also addresses issues beyond energy sources, including urban sprawl and
  agricultural and food systems.

Virginians in our district don't need another climate bill that becomes a
political football. They need an honest plan that takes the problem
seriously, takes the engineering constraints seriously, and is built to
last longer than the next election.
